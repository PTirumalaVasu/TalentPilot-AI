# Rita the Referee - Primary Persona

> Primary target - the person whose daily trust is the actual product metric

**Priority:** PRIMARY 👥
**Role in Flywheel:** THE ENGINE — Rita's behavior change (trusting the dashboard instead of chasing the spreadsheet) is the literal success metric this pilot is measured against
**Created:** 2026-07-08

---

## Profile Summary

**Rita has given up trying to fix the spreadsheet — not because she's given up on the job, but because three years of chasing people for updates taught her that no amount of nagging fixes a chore nobody wants to do.**

She's the HR/L&D admin at SAILS Software, and she opens the shared skills-tracking sheet every single day — not out of habit, but because it's still, today, the only place the information lives. Rita matters to TalentPilot-AI's success more than anyone else in this project, because the entire pilot is a bet on changing one specific thing about her day: whether she can look at a screen and believe what it tells her, without a second thought.

## Background

### Business Journey

**Company Role:** HR/L&D admin at SAILS Software — she owns the "who's ready for what" question for the whole org, not just a training-completion tally.

**Experience Level:** Seasoned in HR process, not a data or engineering background — she thinks in terms of people and readiness, not pipelines or schemas.

**Technical Background:** Comfortable with spreadsheets as a daily tool, but has no expectation that "the system" will be smarter than she is. She trusts what she can see and verify, not what a tool claims about itself.

**Management Style:** Direct and practical. She doesn't manage the employees she tracks — she assigns learning and makes readiness calls that other people (project leads, her own manager) act on. Her job is to be the source everyone else relies on, which is exactly what makes an untrustworthy spreadsheet corrosive to her role, not just annoying.

## Current Situation

### Professional Reality

**The Daily Struggle:**
- Opens the shared Excel sheet daily to add or update must-do skills/courses per employee
- Periodically reviews the status column to judge who's ready for a project — a separate, occasional, higher-stakes act from the daily entry work
- Chases employees who haven't self-reported, which is now a routine (and dreaded) part of the job
- Makes real staffing decisions off data she privately suspects might be stale

**Skills & Tools:**
- Fluent with the shared spreadsheet — no hidden complexity, no personal workaround tabs
- No specialized tracking software; the sheet is genuinely the entire system today
- Comfortable learning a new tool if it's clearly better, but skeptical by default — she's watched "the new system will fix this" promises before

**The Trust Gap:**
- The status column depends entirely on someone else remembering to update it — a single point of failure she has zero control over
- She can't tell, just by looking at a cell, whether "In Progress" means someone actually made progress or just hasn't touched it in three weeks
- What's blocking her: there is currently no way to distinguish a real signal from an unmaintained one without personally following up
- What she needs: a status she doesn't have to independently verify before she can act on it

## Psychological Profile

### Personality & Motivations

**Core Identity:**
- Rita values being the person people can rely on for an accurate answer — that's the actual job, more than "administering training"
- She's **resigned, not tolerant** of the current process — an important distinction: she hasn't decided this is fine, she's decided fighting it further is pointless
- Deep motivation: she wants to make a staffing call and feel confident in it, not just make the call and hope it holds up
- Belief system: data you have to double-check isn't really data — it's a starting point for more work

**Work Style:**
- Rita operates by verification habit: she's learned, from experience, not to take the status column at face value, which means she's quietly doing extra work the tool should be doing for her
- She values clarity over cleverness — a plain "we don't know yet" is more useful to her than a confident-looking cell that turns out to be wrong
- She makes decisions by triangulating: the sheet, plus her own memory of who's been chasing, plus a gut check
- Communication preference: direct, factual, no fluff — she does not want a tool that tries to be delightful about a decision that affects real staffing outcomes

## Driving Forces

### ✅ Top 3 Positive Drivers (What She Wants)

**1. Open the dashboard and simply trust what it says**

- Rita wants to look at a status cell and act on it immediately, without a mental "but is this actually true?" pause first
- Why it matters: every minute spent double-checking is a minute she's doing manual verification that a "system of record" should have already done
- What success looks like: she stops cross-referencing the spreadsheet with her own memory before making a readiness call
- **TalentPilot-AI Promise:** Every verified data point is auto-captured from actual video-watch behavior — no self-report, no lag, no "did someone remember to update this" uncertainty. The label itself says "Verified" so she never has to guess.

**2. Stop chasing people for updates**

- Rita wants the administrative dread of "has anyone actually filled this in?" to simply stop being part of her job
- Why it matters: chasing isn't just annoying — it's an emotional tax she pays daily for something that isn't even her failure
- What success looks like: she goes days without needing to follow up with anyone about a stale status
- **TalentPilot-AI Promise:** Video-based progress updates itself automatically the moment an employee watches — there's no one to chase, because there's no self-report step for her to enforce.

**3. Make a fast, confident readiness call under real pressure**

- When a project needs staffing and someone asks "who's ready," Rita wants to answer quickly and be right
- Why it matters: this is the actual high-stakes moment her whole role exists for — everything else is preparation for this moment
- What success looks like: she filters to "Needs Attention," resolves the handful of real gaps, and gives a confident answer within minutes
- **TalentPilot-AI Promise:** A dedicated "Needs Attention" view surfaces exactly the stale/inconsistent/unclear rows, with drill-down reasoning — so she spends her attention only where it's actually needed, not scanning every row out of habit.

---

### ❌ Top 3 Negative Drivers (What She Fears)

**1. Making a staffing call on data that turns out to be wrong**

- Rita's deepest fear isn't the spreadsheet itself — it's being the person who confidently said "they're ready" and was wrong, in front of people who acted on her word
- Why this terrifies her: her professional credibility is built on being the reliable source; one bad call built on stale data undermines that identity
- What failure looks like: she staffs someone onto a project based on a status that was actually three weeks old and untrue
- **TalentPilot-AI Answer:** The mixed-trust data model never blends verified and self-reported into one indistinguishable signal — every cell states its own provenance and freshness explicitly ("Verified · 92% watched, 2 hours ago" vs. "Self-reported · Not updated in 14 days"), so she never mistakes an unverified guess for a fact.

**2. A dashboard that looks more trustworthy than it actually is**

- Rita is specifically wary of a tool that replaces her honest doubt about the old spreadsheet with false confidence in a new one — that would be worse than the status quo, not better
- Why this terrifies her: at least the old spreadsheet's unreliability was something she'd learned to compensate for; a slick new tool that hides the same unreliability behind clean design could cause her to stop compensating and get burned
- What failure looks like: TalentPilot-AI shows a uniform, polished-looking grid, and she starts trusting cells she shouldn't
- **TalentPilot-AI Answer:** Deliberately labels every data point as verified, self-reported, or needs-attention rather than a single trust-level UI — the product's own positioning explicitly rejects "universally trustworthy" in favor of "honest about what it can and can't verify."

**3. The chore just moves, instead of disappearing**

- Rita fears that "automated tracking" quietly still depends on someone remembering to do something manually somewhere else in the system — same failure mode, new interface
- Why this terrifies her: she's watched process-improvement efforts before that just relabeled the old problem without solving it
- What failure looks like: video tracking works fine, but sub-skills and non-video assignments are still silently self-reported and stale, and she doesn't notice until she's burned by one of them
- **TalentPilot-AI Answer:** Non-video signal gaps are shown as explicit "No activity recorded yet" or "Self-reported" labels rather than blended into a false-positive-looking status — a blank or unknown cell beats a guessed one, by explicit design principle.

## Role in Flywheel: Making the Honest Data Model Work for the Person Who Lives With It Daily

Rita represents the person TalentPilot-AI exists to serve first — not because she's a "customer" in a commercial sense, but because her daily trust *is* the pilot's entire success criterion. There's no separate "convert Rita" step; there's only "does she, in practice, stop reaching for the spreadsheet."

**The Natural Evolution:**
1. Rita assigns a skill in TalentPilot-AI instead of the shared sheet — the first small behavior switch
2. She sees her first "Verified · X% watched, N hours ago" cell update without anyone telling her to check it
3. She tries the "Needs Attention" filter once, out of curiosity, and it correctly surfaces a real gap
4. She uses "Needs Attention" as her default readiness-judgment habit, not a novelty feature
5. She answers a real staffing question fast and confidently, and it holds up
6. She stops opening the legacy spreadsheet — not because she was told to, but because it stopped being useful to her

## What Rita Needs to See on the Dashboard

**1. Immediate, unambiguous provenance on every cell**
- Every status paired with its label: verified / self-reported / needs attention
- No color-only signals — always paired with text
- Key message: "You will never have to guess what kind of data this is."

**2. A "Needs Attention" view that's actually useful, not decorative**
- Surfaces genuinely stale/inconsistent/unclear rows, not just "recently touched"
- Drill-down reasoning available on click, not buried
- Key message: "This is where your actual attention should go today."

**3. Freshness stated in plain language, not vague status words**
- "Not updated in 14 days" instead of "In Progress"
- No ambiguous terms that could mean either verified or self-reported
- Key message: "You will never mistake a stale cell for a fresh one."

**4. A visible answer to "why is this person marked ready"**
- One-click drill-down showing the underlying signal (e.g., "92% watched, 2 hours ago")
- Key message: "You can defend this call if someone questions it."

**5. Confirmation that the assignment flow and the dashboard are the same continuous system**
- Assigning a skill and later checking its status feel like one workflow, not two disconnected tools
- Key message: "This isn't a second system you have to maintain — it's the same place you already work."
