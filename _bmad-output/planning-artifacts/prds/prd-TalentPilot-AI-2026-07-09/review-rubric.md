# PRD Quality Review — TalentPilot-AI

## Overall verdict

This is an unusually rigorous PRD: every FR carries testable consequences with real thresholds, the Vision and personas are specific to SAILS' actual spreadsheet failure rather than generic template filler, and the doc uses `[ASSUMPTION]` / `[NOTE FOR PM]` / Open Questions honestly rather than as decoration. The one defect that could cause real downstream harm is mechanical, not rhetorical: FR-12 (HR Override) is fully specified in §4.4 and explicitly called MVP in the §6.2 Out-of-Scope note, but is absent from the §6.1 In-Scope FR enumeration — a story-writer scanning that list alone would drop it. A second gap is that FR-3, the one FR governing AI content-matching quality, is the only FR with no "Consequences (testable)" block. Fix those two and this PRD is close to as good as the format gets.

## Decision-readiness — strong

Trade-offs are named with what was given up, not smoothed to neutral. FR-1's "[+ New Assignment]" flow decision to allow assigning without content (FR-2: "the flow allows the HR Admin to assign the Skill without Content, rather than blocking the assignment") is a stated decision, not a hedge. `[NOTE FOR PM]` callouts land at genuine open tensions rather than safe checkpoints — §4.2's ingestion-cadence note ("This caps how quickly the Content catalog can grow or refresh") and §4.4's FR-12 note ("No existing UX scenario or prototype covers this interaction") both flag real unresolved work, not rhetorical questions with the answer next door. The Manager/Team-Lead role question is explicitly closed (§2.2: "This was left as an open question in an earlier draft brief and is now resolved").

### Findings
- **medium** Compressed timeline risk is disclosed but under-elevated (Constraints and Guardrails; Open Question 7) — The PRD's front matter and Constraints section assert launch on **2026-07-13**, four days after the PRD's own `created: 2026-07-09` date, with only static HTML prototypes built so far (addendum, "Prototype Implementation Notes": "All three UX scenarios... were built as static HTML prototypes") and the full FastAPI/React/Postgres stack ("locked" per addendum) apparently unbuilt. The tension is named — Open Question 7 says "Given the hard 2026-07-13 launch date, this is a real risk to the date holding, not just a remaining implementation nicety" — but it's buried as item 7 of 8 Open Questions rather than surfaced next to the date assertion itself in Constraints and Guardrails, where a decision-maker would first hit the commitment. *Fix:* cross-reference Open Question 7 directly from the Constraints and Guardrails launch-date bullet, or promote it above the fold.

## Substance over theater — strong

No persona padding: exactly two personas (Rita, Casey), and both drive multiple FRs and UJs directly rather than existing for coverage. The Vision (§1) is anchored to SAILS' specific spreadsheet mechanics ("HR assigns must-do skills, employees are supposed to self-report progress... By the time HR needs an answer... the data can't be trusted") — it would not swap cleanly into another company's PRD, which is the test for vision theater. Cross-Cutting NFRs avoid boilerplate: "Readiness Dashboard loads in under 2 seconds," "dashboard rows reflect a new watch-position update within 30 seconds," WCAG 2.1 AA with concrete behavioral requirements (never color-only, keyboard-operable, screen-reader announcements for live updates) — these are bounds, not adjectives. "Why Now" cites specific, sourced stats (87% CHRO AI-adoption expectation, WEF 6-in-10 upskilling projection) rather than an unearned differentiation claim. No findings.

## Strategic coherence — strong

The thesis is explicit and singular: replace self-reported status with automatic capture "for the one content type where it's possible: video" (§1), and every in-scope feature (assignment, discovery, capture/resume, provenance dashboard) serves that thesis directly — nothing reads as backlog-with-headings. Counter-metrics are present and specific: SM-C1 guards against a false-positive read of SM-1 ("HR Admin continuing to privately cross-reference the old spreadsheet... after go-live is a red flag even if SM-1's backend number looks good"), and SM-C2 guards against a surveillance-perception failure mode independent of SM-3's raw capture-rate number.

### Findings
- **medium** SM-5 has no quantified target, unlike its siblings (§7) — SM-1 ("drops below 5%"), SM-3 ("100%"), and SM-4 ("under 2 minutes") are each falsifiable; SM-5 ("Employee video completion rate and Continue-Watching... usage — adoption signals") states no threshold, so there's no defined point at which it's judged to have passed or failed. *Fix:* add a numeric bound (e.g., "≥X% of assigned videos resumed at least once within 7 days") or explicitly mark it observational-only like SM-2.

## Done-ness clarity — thin (isolated to one FR, otherwise strong)

Nearly every FR is unforgiving in the way the rubric wants: FR-7's "Consequences (testable)" specifies the exact two-tab race condition and the required outcome; FR-6 distinguishes a launch-blocking defect ("a wrong resume point on a Employee's first encounter... is treated as a launch-blocking defect, not a minor bug") from ordinary bugs; FR-8 replaces "In Progress"-style ambiguity with "Not updated in 14 days." This is genuinely done-ness-oriented writing, not adjective-driven.

### Findings
- **medium** FR-3 has no testable consequences (§4.2) — Every other core FR (FR-1, FR-2, FR-5, FR-6, FR-7, FR-8, FR-9, FR-10, FR-11, FR-12) has an explicit "Consequences (testable)" block; FR-3 — the FR governing AI semantic content-matching, arguably the highest-uncertainty piece of the system — has only a description and an "Out of Scope" bullet. "Loose/approximate topical matches are acceptable; exact tag matching is not required" is not falsifiable as written: there's no similarity threshold, no defined "good enough" bar, and no behavior specified for a low-confidence match (as opposed to FR-2's already-covered zero-match case). An engineer can't tell what "done" looks like for the matching quality itself. *Fix:* add a testable bound — e.g., a minimum cosine-similarity threshold, or a lightweight human-relevance spot-check process before pilot launch.

## Scope honesty — strong

Non-Goals (§5) does real work with seven specific, reasoned exclusions rather than a boilerplate disclaimer. De-scoping is proposed honestly rather than silently: "Dedicated 'Needs Attention' filter control — considered... and explicitly not added; FR-9's per-row drill-down plus FR-10's visual flagging is the MVP interaction model instead" (§6.2) names the alternative that was rejected and why. The Assumptions Index (§9) round-trips cleanly — all three inline `[ASSUMPTION]` tags (§2.1, §4.1, §4.2) have matching index entries, and the §4.1 entry goes further than the inline tag, noting the decision "knowingly supersede[s]" an earlier PRFAQ commitment to a content-approval checkpoint — an honest reversal, not a silent one.

### Findings
- **low** Open-item density is high for a doc framed as build-ready (§0: "authoritative capability spec" for "whoever picks up architecture and implementation work") — 8 Open Questions + 3 `[ASSUMPTION]`s + 3 `[NOTE FOR PM]` callouts = 14 disclosed open items against a launch date four days out from the PRD's own creation date. Every item is genuinely disclosed (this is a scope-honesty strength, not a violation), but the volume itself — deployment target undecided, no named post-pilot owner, legal review skipped, root-cause hypothesis unvalidated, provenance-label comprehension untested — is worth a reader pausing on before treating this as fully green-lit. *Fix:* none required structurally; consider a one-line "readiness caveat" up top pointing at Open Questions 3, 6, 7, 8 as the ones that matter most before build starts.

## Downstream usability — strong

Glossary (§3) is used consistently: "HR Admin," "Employee," "Skill," "Content," "Assignment," and "Provenance Label" all appear identically capitalized across Features, Non-Goals, MVP Scope, and Success Metrics. FR IDs (FR-1–FR-12), UJ IDs (UJ-1–UJ-3), and SM IDs (SM-1–SM-5, SM-C1–SM-C2) are contiguous with no duplicates. Every UJ has a named protagonist carrying context inline (Rita, Casey, Rita) — no floating UJs. Cross-references generally resolve: FR-12's "(§3 Glossary)," UJ-1's "(see SM-C1, §7)."

### Findings
- **high** FR-12 is missing from the §6.1 MVP In-Scope FR list — §6.1 enumerates "FR-1, FR-2 / FR-3, FR-4 / FR-5, FR-6, FR-7 / FR-8, FR-9, FR-10, FR-11" and stops there. FR-12 (HR Override) is fully specified in §4.4 with its own testable consequences and is explicitly confirmed as MVP in the §6.2 Out-of-Scope note — "(The related manual-override safety net shipped in MVP as FR-12, not deferred — see §4.4.)" — but that confirmation lives in the *Out of Scope* section, one click away from where a downstream reader would actually look for the MVP FR list. Anyone deriving a build checklist mechanically from §6.1 alone would drop FR-12 despite it being explicitly promoted from a v2 candidate to an MVP commitment "per PRFAQ commitment reconciliation" (§4.4). *Fix:* add FR-12 to §6.1's In-Scope list, e.g. under the Dashboard bullet: "(FR-8, FR-9, FR-10, FR-11, FR-12)."

## Shape fit — strong

This is a two-role internal B2B tool with a meaningful workflow judgment at its center (Rita's readiness call), which is exactly the shape where named-protagonist UJs are load-bearing per the rubric — and the PRD uses exactly three, no more. It doesn't over-formalize (no UJs manufactured for Employee beyond the one that matters) and doesn't under-formalize (a consumer-product-lite UX like the dashboard and resume flow genuinely needed UJ treatment, and got it). The PRD correctly identifies itself as chain-top (§0: "for whoever picks up architecture and implementation work against it") and treats downstream usability accordingly, while pushing implementation-only detail (stack, rejected alternatives) into `addendum.md` rather than bloating the PRD itself — a good shape decision, not template compliance. No findings.

## Mechanical notes

- **Glossary drift on watch-tracking terminology.** §3 defines two distinct terms — **Watch Progress** (a percentage) and **Resume Position** (a timestamp) — but FR-5's consequences use "Watch position... is sampled," FR-7 uses "watch-position update," and FR-11 uses "new watch-position update." None of these match either Glossary term exactly, and it's not clear from context whether "watch position" means Watch Progress, Resume Position, or a merged concept of both. Downstream FR/data-model extraction (the addendum's `skill_progress` table conflates both concepts into one row) would benefit from either aliasing "watch position" explicitly to one Glossary term or defining it as a third term.
- **Section numbering breaks after §7.** Headings past "## 7. Success Metrics" — Cross-Cutting NFRs, Constraints and Guardrails, Why Now, Open Questions, Assumptions Index — carry no visible section numerals, yet body text cross-references them numerically: Non-Goals (§5) references content-approval as "(§8)," and Document Purpose (§0) states assumptions are "indexed in §9." A reader can't resolve "§8" or "§9" by scanning headers; they have to count unlabeled sections to infer that "§8" = Constraints and Guardrails and "§9" = Assumptions Index. *Fix:* either number the remaining headings (## 8. Cross-Cutting NFRs, etc.) or switch the numeric cross-refs to name-based ones (as the Glossary's "Coaching-only" entry already does correctly: "see §Constraints and Guardrails").
- **Assumptions Index roundtrip: clean.** All three inline `[ASSUMPTION]` tags (§2.1, §4.1, §4.2) have matching, non-orphaned index entries with no extras.
- **ID continuity: clean except the §6.1 gap above.** FR-1 through FR-12 contiguous and unique; UJ-1–UJ-3 and SM-1–SM-5/SM-C1–SM-C2 contiguous and unique.
